The neural crest is a mesenchymal cell population that arises alongside the neural tube in vertebrate embryos. Neural crest cells then migrate throughout the embryo and become many different cell types. One mechanism that is important in controlling the directional migration of neural crest cells through the embryo is "contact inhibition of locomotion". Contact inhibition of locomotion occurs when a cell, upon contact with another cell, reorients and migrates in a different direction.

As a PhD student I characterised the localisation and function of Pard3 in neural crest cells. Pard3 is a polarity protein, which means that it is usually located to only one side (or "pole") of a cell. We knew that Pard3 was located to cell-cell contacts between epithelial cells and to the "leading edge" (front) of cells that migrate by themselves, but we didn't know what it might be doing in neural crests cells that migrate but also have cell-cell contacts. I found that Pard3 also localised to cell-cell contacts in the migrating neural crest cells. When two cells touched, Pard3 was localised to the contact site and promoted microtubule catastrophe (indirectly, by inhibiting the Rac-GEF Trio), causing the cell to migrate away from the site of cell-cell contact. Altogether, this meanse that Pard3 is important in contact inhibition of locomotion and so in neural crest cell migration.
