We usually think of RNA splicing proteins as being in the nucleus, where they splice mRNA molecules for export from the nucleus. Once out of the nucleus, the cell uses mRNAs as templates to make proteins. RNA splicing proteins are important because, by changing how they splice the mRNAs - called "alternative splicing" - they  massively increase the variety of proteins available to the cell.

In neurons, some mRNAs are specifically transported to axons or dendrites, and this is essential for proper neuronal development. We now know that alternative splicing of mRNAs regulates neuronal maturation and connectivity. Recent work in the Houart lab has shown that two RNA splicing proteins - sfpq and snrnp70 - are both localised to growing and maturing axons _in vivo_. Cytoplasmic sfpq and snrnp70 both modulate the types of mRNAs in the axon and impact axonal shape and connectivity.

When I joined the Houart lab, my aim was to assess how general this phenomenon is. We screened for other RNA splicing proteins localised to axons and found members of the SRSF (serine/arginine splicing factor) family in axons throughout the brain and spinal cord during neuronal development. However, SRSF family proteins are generally expressed in every cell throughout development, which makes it difficult to figure out their role specifically in neurons, let alone specifically in the cytoplasm. They are also tightly regulated, so we want to focus on the endogenous protein and avoid overexpression if possible.

To get around these challenges, we are using the ALFA/ALFA-nanobody system. I have generated zebrafish lines where _endogenous_ srsf1a, srsf1b or srsf3a carry an ALFA tag. I then use fluorescently-tagged ALFA-nanobody to visualise endogenous protein localisation and dynamics in axons in live zebrafish embryos. Alternatively, I use a degron-tagged ALFA-nanobody to degrade endogenous srsf1a or srsf3a only in certain cells and/or cellular compartments. I am now using the same nanobody fusion approach to isolate the protein complexes formed by SRSF proteins in axon and understand the influence of SRSF proteins on the local mRNAs.
