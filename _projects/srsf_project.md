We usually think of RNA splicing proteins as being in the nucleus, where they (believe it or not!) splice mRNA molecules so they can be exported from the nucleus. Once out of the nucleus, the cell uses mRNAs as templates to make proteins. RNA splicing proteins are important because, by changing how they splice the mRNAs - called "alternative splicing" - they  massively increase the variety of proteins available to the cell.

In neurons, some mRNAs are specifically transported to axons or dendrites, and this is essential for proper neuronal development. We now know that alternative splicing of mRNAs regulates neuronal maturation and connectivity. Recent work in the Houart lab has shown that two RNA splicing proteins - sfpq and snrnp70 - are both localised to growing and maturing axons _in vivo_. Cytoplasmic sfpq and snrnp70 both modulate the types of mRNAs in the axon and impact axonal shape and connectivity.

When I joined the Houart lab, my aim was to assess how general this phenomenon is. We screened for other RNA splicing proteins localised to axons and found that members of the SRSF (serine/arginine splicing factor) family in axons throughout the brain and spinal cord during neuronal development. To focus specifically on the axonal function of these proteins, we are using the ALFA/ALFA-nanobody system to visualise endogenous srsf1a and srsf3a protein localisation and dynamics in axons in live zebrafish embryos, and a degron-nanobody to degrade endogenous proteins only in certain cells and/or cellular compartments. I am now using the same nanobody fusion approach to isolate the protein complexes that SRSFs are forming in the axons and understand the influence of SRSF proteins on the local mRNAs.
