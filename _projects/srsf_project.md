The localisation of mRNAs to axons and dendrites is essential for proper neuronal development, and mRNA alternative splicing is now understood to regulate neuronal maturation and connectivity. Recent work in the Houart lab has shown that two RNA splicing proteins - sfpq and the U1 spliceosome protein snrnp70 - are both localised to growing and maturing axon _in vivo_, where they modulate the local transcriptome and control axonal shape and connectivity.

To assess how general this phenomenon is, we screened for other axonal spliceosome proteins and found that members of the SRSF (serine/arginine splicing factor) family are localised to axons throughout the brain and spinal cord during neuronal development. To specifically focus on the axonal function of these proteins, we are using the ALFA/ALFA-nanobody system to visualise endogenout srsf1a and srsf3 protein localisation and dynamics in axons in live zebrafish embryos, and a degron-nanobody to degrade endogenous proteins only in certain cells and/or cellular compartments. The same nanobody fusion approach will be used to isolate the protein complexes that SRSFs are forming in the axons and dissect the influence of these proteins on the local transcriptome. This three-pronged strategy will elucidate the role of SRSF proteins locally in developing neurites. We will present the advances we have made in both imaging and degrading endogenous axonal SRSF proteins.
