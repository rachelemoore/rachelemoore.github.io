Nerve cells connect to their targets - such as muscles or other neurons - via long thin processes called axons. During embryonic development, newborn neurons extend a specific number of axons from specific positions on the cell body. This is essential for meurons to connect and form a functioning nervous system, but once development is complete, neurons in the mammalian central nervous system (CNS - the brain and spinal cord) usually can't regrow an axon after it's damaged, even when the cell body remains healthy. Axon initiation is important for both embryology and CNS repair, but we still don't fully understand it.

Axon initiation has mostly been studied in cultured neurons. During my first post-doc I established the zebrafish embryonic spinal cord as an alternative experimental model. Zebrafish embryos are transparent, making it easy to see living neurons developing within their normal environment. The complex 3D environment of the embryo illustrated novel aspects of this process. I found that neurons in the zebrafish spinal cord extend one or three axons directly from stereotyped positions on the cell body. My results also challenged the common view that microtubules are required for axon initiation.

In  current models it is difficult to distinguish axon initiation from axonal growth or neuronal migration. In the embryonic zebrafish spinal cord neurons do not migrate and axons grow directly from the soma, allowing me to study axon initiation independently of axon growth or neuronal migration. Because axon initiation also occurs at a predictable position on the cell body, I can pinpoint exactly when and where axon initiation will occur. In the future I will take advantage of these significant technical advantages as well as recent advantages in expansion microscopy, in vivo optogenetics and proteomics to address questions about axon initiation that were previously technicially impossible, uncovering the cellular and molecular mechanisms driving axon initiation in vivo.
